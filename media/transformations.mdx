---
title: "Transformations"
description: "What Postrun adjusts in your file so each platform accepts it — and what it deliberately leaves untouched."
---

When you upload a file, Postrun prepares a version that each target platform will
accept, doing the least work needed. You don't run ffmpeg or an image pipeline, and
you don't have to know any platform's rules. Here's what that means for images and
video.

## Images

Postrun delivers a version that fits each target:

- **Resizes** down to the platform's dimension limit, keeping the aspect ratio.
- **Converts** to the format the platform expects — JPEG for the social platforms,
  PNG where transparency needs to be preserved.
- **Recompresses** to stay under the platform's file-size cap.
- **Rasterizes** formats the platforms can't accept at all (AVIF, TIFF, SVG) into a
  standard one.

## Video

Postrun makes the file playable everywhere while avoiding needless re-encoding:

- **Re-encodes the codec only when a target requires it** — for example, an iPhone
  HEVC clip becomes H.264 for X, which only accepts H.264. If every target already
  accepts the source codec, Postrun **re-wraps the file without re-encoding** — fast,
  and with no quality loss.
- **Optimizes it for instant playback**, so platforms don't stall while loading.
- **Normalizes the technical details** a codec needs (such as even dimensions and
  frame-rate bounds).

GIFs and documents pass through unchanged — there's nothing to convert; Postrun only
validates them.

## What Postrun never changes

Postrun adjusts the technical envelope — format, codec, size — **never your
content**. It doesn't crop, trim, or restage your media. If a clip is longer than a
platform allows, or an image's aspect ratio falls outside a platform's range,
Postrun doesn't silently cut it — it reports the problem so you can decide what to
do (see [Media Response](/media/media-response)).

## Knowing what was adjusted

Every change Postrun makes is reported in the asset's per-platform **`warnings`** —
for example, that a video was re-encoded, or an image recompressed to fit. The
rendition is ready to use; the warning is just there if you want to know what
happened.
